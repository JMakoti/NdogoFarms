Companion Guide

What is Companion Planting?

Companion planting is the practice of growing two or more plant species in close proximity to provide mutual benefits such as pest control, improved pollination, better use of space, soil improvement, and higher yields. The practice is both traditional and increasingly supported by agroecological research and practical experience.

Key Principles and Mechanisms

Biodiversity & Ecological Balance

Increasing plant diversity supports beneficial insects and predators, reducing pest outbreaks and creating a more resilient garden ecosystem.

Attraction of Beneficial Insects

Flowering herbs and flowers attract pollinators and predators like ladybirds and lacewings that help control pests.

Trap Cropping

Plant sacrificial crops that draw pests away from the main crop (e.g., nasturtiums for aphids).

Physical Support & Shade

Taller crops provide shade or structural support for vines like beans or cucumbers.

Nitrogen Fixation & Soil Improvement

Legumes fix nitrogen and improve fertility for neighboring heavy feeders like brassicas and leafy greens.

Allelopathy and Chemical Interactions

Some plants release chemicals that suppress or deter pests or weeds; understand which plants may inhibit others.

Spatial & Temporal Complementarity

Pair deep-rooted with shallow-rooted plants or stagger plantings to maximize resource use.

Benefits of Companion Planting

  • Reduced pest pressure without reliance on synthetic pesticides
  • Improved pollination and higher yields for fruiting crops
  • Better space utilization in small urban gardens
  • Enhanced soil fertility and structure over time
  • Diversified harvests and reduced risk of total crop failure

Design Patterns for Small / Urban Gardens

Three Sisters (Native American Model)

Corn, beans and squash grown together: corn provides support for beans, beans fix nitrogen, squash suppresses weeds and shades soil.

Herb & Vegetable Edges

Plant herbs and flowers around vegetable beds to attract beneficial insects and repel pests.

Vertical Layering

Use vertical spaces for vining crops; place low-growing herbs at edges to maximize light and airflow.

Trap Crop Rows

Plant sacrificial rows of nasturtiums or radishes at the garden edge to draw pests away from main crops.

Kenya-Specific Notes

Climate zones vary across Kenya. For urban Nairobi gardeners, focus on:

  • Using legumes (beans/peas) to improve soil for leafy vegetables like sukuma wiki
  • Planting marigolds and strong-scented herbs to reduce nematodes and pests
  • Timing plantings around the long rains (March-May) and short rains (Oct-Dec); stagger plantings to ensure continuous harvests

Local Resources: GrowPact Kenya and KALRO provide region-specific calendars and companion suggestions.

  2. Observe and record: Note pest pressure, yields, and plant health weekly
  3. Rotate crops yearly to reduce disease buildup
  4. Use flowering strips or pots with pollinator-attracting plants
